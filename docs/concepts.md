# Concepts

Catalog of workspace design philosophies, presented as **options** for evaluation. No decisions made yet.

---

## 1. Robert Propst — *The Office: A Facility Based on Change* (1968)

**Origin:** Research at Herman Miller with environmental psychologists and behavioral scientists. The book that inspired the Action Office system — and was later distorted into the cubicle.

**Core idea:** Workspaces must change as work changes. The facility is a living system, not a fixed floor plan.

**Key principles:**
- **120° angles, never 90°** — Right angles are building geometry, not human geometry. Three panels at 120° create a partial enclosure that preserves peripheral vision, creates natural entry/exit flow, and eliminates dead corners.
- **Postural variation** — 8 hours in a chair degrades body and cognition. Each station should offer multiple surface heights: low table for seated focus, high bench for brief standing meetings, shelves for leaning/moving.
- **Vertical surfaces as mental extension** — Walls and panels around the station are for pinning, displaying, organizing — not decoration. Information externalization through the visual field.
- **Gradual privacy** — Not binary (closed room vs. open plan). A gradient: high privacy for deep focus, medium for connected concentration, low for collaboration.
- **Radical adaptability** — "Facility based on change" is the principle AND the title. Work changes, teams change, projects change; space reconfigures in hours, not construction.
- **Task differentiation** — Uniform benches are a mistake. Different tasks need different ergonomics, lighting, tools, and privacy levels. The office should look more like a professional kitchen (specialized stations) than a classroom (identical rows).
- **Functional egalitarianism** — Against the "corner office = power" model. Everyone deserves functional space for their actual task; nobody deserves trophy-space for status.

**What went wrong:** Companies froze the layouts to save money. The modular system became static cubicles — exactly the opposite of what Propst intended.

**Pros for LaRA:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with behavioral research
- Directly applicable to bench and lab design
- The 120° principle creates natural collaboration bays

**Cons / Critiques:**
- Designed for office knowledge work, not physical making
- Doesn't address dust, fumes, ESD, heavy equipment
- The cubicle failure shows good principles can be corrupted by cost-cutting

**Further reading:** Herman Miller official page on the work: https://www.hermanmiller.com/stories/why-magazine/the-office-documented/

## 2. Bürolandschaft (Quickborner Team, Hamburg, 1960s)

**Origin:** Eberhard and Wolfgang Schnelle developed this in Germany as a response to rigid corporate layouts. Inspired by landscape design — organic, flowing, non-grid.

**Core idea:** Layout follows the actual flow of information and communication between people, not architectural symmetry. Map who talks to whom, then arrange desks accordingly.

**Key principles:**
- **Organic layout** — No grid. No straight rows. Desks placed according to real communication patterns.
- **Flow-based arrangement** — Map paper/information flow before designing space. Result is almost never symmetrical.
- **Plants and curved dividers** — Break straight lines with biophilic elements and curved screens.
- **Landscape metaphor** — Office as a landscape with hills (partial screens), paths (circulation), clearings (meeting areas).

**Pros for LaRA:**
- Relevant for a lab with 30+ students where collaboration patterns matter
- Encourages mapping actual workflows before committing to layout
- Anti-grid philosophy pairs well with Propst's 120° principle

**Cons / Critiques:**
- Requires detailed study of communication patterns first
- Can look messy/unstructured to traditional eyes
- Harder to implement in a small 7×7m space — needs room for organic flow

**Reference:** Look for photos of the Buch und Ton office (1961) and Bertelsmann headquarters.

## 3. OpenStructures (Thomas Lommée, 2009–present)

**Origin:** Belgian designer Thomas Lommée created an open modular building system based on a universal 4×4 cm grid.

**Core idea:** Define a universal grid standard. Anyone can design components that connect to anyone else's components. Interoperability by design.

**Key principles:**
- **Universal grid (4×4 cm)** — All components share the same mounting pattern
- **Interoperability** — Any bench connects to any shelf connects to any panel
- **Open standard** — Anyone can design new parts that fit the system
- **Cumulative design** — The system grows as more people contribute

**Pros for LaRA:**
- Could define a grid standard for the lab (e.g., 40×40 cm, or multiples of V-slot profile)
- Student-designed benches would automatically be compatible
- Aligns with open hardware philosophy

**Cons / Critiques:**
- The 4×4 cm grid may be too small for furniture-scale work
- Requires discipline to maintain standard compliance
- Less documentation/examples than traditional approaches

**Website:** https://openstructures.net

## 4. Open Source Ecology — Global Village Construction Set (Marcin Jakubowski)

**Origin:** Marcin Jakubowski's project to create 50 open-source industrial machines for building civilization from scratch.

**Core idea:** The lab builds its own lab. Infrastructure is self-replicating and open.

**Key principles:**
- **Self-replication** — Machines that can make other machines
- **Open documentation** — Complete build instructions, BOM, CAD files
- **Modular** — Components shared across machines
- **Distributed production** — Anyone can build anywhere

**Pros for LaRA:**
- First student cohort could build the lab furniture as their initiation project
- CNC can cut bench parts — the lab literally makes itself
- Strong documentation culture from the start

**Cons / Critiques:**
- More focused on heavy industrial equipment than precision lab work
- Aesthetic is utilitarian, not refined
- Time investment before having usable infrastructure

**Website:** https://opensourceecology.org

## 5. Cedric Price — Fun Palace (1961, unbuilt)

**Origin:** Architect Cedric Price designed a building where walls, floors, and stairs would be reconfigured daily by cranes based on the activity. Never built, but hugely influential.

**Core idea:** Architecture as infrastructure, not form. The building is a kit of parts; the users decide the configuration.

**Key principles:**
- **Everything reconfigurable** — Nothing fixed. Walls, floors, stairs change daily.
- **Infrastructure as service** — Building provides power, water, structure. Users provide the layout.
- **Time-based design** — Space changes over time by design, not despite design.
- **Anti-monument** — The building should be unremarkable; the activity is what matters.

**Pros for LaRA:**
- Radical version of "everything on casters" philosophy
- Validates the idea that lab layout should change with each project/semester
- The "infrastructure as service" model fits well with overhead power/air drops

**Cons / Critiques:**
- Extreme — never actually built even by its designer
- Requires significant overhead infrastructure (power rails, cable management)
- Not practical for heavy equipment (CNC, 3D printers)

**Influence:** Centre Pompidou, MIT Media Lab.

## 6. Lina Bo Bardi — SESC Pompéia (São Paulo, 1977–86)

**Origin:** Brazilian architect transformed a drum factory into a community center with workshops, library, sports, theater. All furniture designed to be reconfigured by users.

**Core idea:** Workspace as public, living space. No hierarchy between activities. Users reshape the environment.

**Key principles:**
- **User reconfiguration** — Furniture designed to be moved, stacked, rearranged by non-experts
- **No hierarchy** — Woodworking, ceramics, printing all share equal space and dignity
- **Industrial heritage** — Embrace the raw character of industrial spaces
- **Social space** — Work is public and communal, not hidden in back rooms

**Pros for LaRA:**
- Brazilian reference with rich documentation (Instituto Lina Bo e P.M. Bardi)
- Anti-cubicle philosophy with warmth and humanity
- Shows that lab can be visible/performative (glass walls, open to corridor)

**Cons / Critiques:**
- Community center context is different from university research lab
- Aesthetic may be too rustic for precision electronics work
- Less focused on individual workstation ergonomics

## 7. Salk Institute — Louis Kahn (1965)

**Origin:** Jonas Salk (polio vaccine) commissioned Louis Kahn to design a biological research institute. The result is almost monastic — labs as contemplative space.

**Core idea:** "Served vs. servant spaces." Research spaces (served) are clean, open, flexible. Infrastructure spaces (servant — ducts, pipes, power) are separated into interstitial floors.

**Key principles:**
- **Served vs. servant** — Separate where you work from where the infrastructure lives
- **Contemplative quality** — Research benefits from architectural quality and beauty
- **Flexible open labs** — Large open spaces that can be subdivided as needed
- **Infrastructure in interstitial floors** — Power, air, data accessible without entering the lab

**Pros for LaRA:**
- The "served vs. servant" idea applies even at bench scale (cable management trays, overhead rails)
- Validates investing in architectural quality for a research space
- Open lab concept with subdivision potential

**Cons / Critiques:**
- Monumental scale doesn't translate directly to a 7×7m room
- Budget reality of a university lab vs. a Salk-commissioned building
- Interstitial floors are not feasible in most existing buildings

## 8. Activity-Based Working (ABW)

**Origin:** Modern workplace strategy (Veldhoen + Company, 1990s) where employees choose from a variety of settings based on the task at hand.

**Core idea:** No assigned desks. Instead, a menu of activity settings — each optimized for a specific type of work.

**Key principles:**
- **Task-based settings** — Library zone, collaboration zone, focus booth, standing bench, lounge
- **Choice** — Workers select the environment that matches their current activity
- **Shared resources** — Everyone has access to everything; nothing is "owned"
- **Density optimization** — Because not everyone uses every space simultaneously, total footprint is efficient

**Pros for LaRA:**
- Maps well to a lab with diverse activities (soldering, coding, assembly, testing)
- Could define distinct zones in the 7×7m space
- Reduces the "my desk" territoriality problem in shared labs

**Cons / Critiques:**
- Requires enough variety of settings to actually offer choice
- Storage/personal items need separate solution when there are no assigned desks
- Can feel impersonal without personal territory
